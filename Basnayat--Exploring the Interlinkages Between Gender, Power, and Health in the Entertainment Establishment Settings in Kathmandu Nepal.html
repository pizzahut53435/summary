Research Article

Exploring the Interlinkages Between Gender, Power, and Health in the Entertainment Establishment Settings in Kathmandu Nepal

Iccha Basnyat1

Abstract

Qualitative Health Research 2020, Vol. 30(9) 1409 1418 The Author(s) 2020 Article reuse guidelines: sagepub.com/journals-permissions https://doi.org/10.1177/1049732320913854 DOI 10.1177/1049732320913854 journals.sagepub.com/home/qhr

This article explores the ways in which gender inequalities are reproduced and reinforced within entertainment establishments, and in turn the ways in which it affects health of the women working in these establishments. Specifically, this article is based on 35 in-depth interviews with women working in entertainment establishments in Kathmandu, Nepal. The findings highlight daily encounters of harassment by customers demanding engagement and acceptance of various sexual advances; strategies of exploitation by employers such as threatening the loss of work and/or salary to coerce the women into engaging in sexual talks and innuendoes to increase sales for the business; and an adverse impact on the physical, emotional, and psychological health of women working in the entertainment establishment. The findings urge us to locate the discussion of health, particularly health of marginalized women, within the context of gender inequalities, labor divisions, and power relations.

Keywords

entertainment establishments; gender; health; inequalities; interviews; Nepal; power

Existing research has established that, across the world, women employed in entertainment establishments such as karaoke bars, dance/night clubs, exotic dancing/strip clubs, hotels, and massage parlors often face increased risk of physical abuse, alcohol and drug abuse, sexually transmitted disease (STI)/human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), stigma, sexual assault, social marginalization, ostracism, exploitation, harassment, psychological and emotional strain, while having limited access to preven tive measures (see, for instance, Choudhury et al., 2015; Maticka-Tyndale et al., 2000; Nemoto et al., 2008; Seib et al., 2009; Wong et al., 2012; Yi et al., 2015). For instance, Rueben et al., (2011) found in Baltimore high levels of transactional sex and drug use among exotic dancers. In Canada, Maticka-Tyndale et al. (2000) con cluded that women working in entertainment establish ments faced threats to their health such as increased STI/ HIV prevalence, alcohol and drug abuse, increased psy chological and emotional strain, sexual assault, and skin cancer related to tanning. Similarly, Nemoto et al. (2008) found in Vietnam a decreased level of condom use among female karaoke bar workers leading to greater STI/HIV risk and prevalence. In Cambodia, new HIV infections and challenges to the sexual reproductive health of entertainment establishmentbased women workers have been found (Yi et al., 2015).

Entertainment establishment are places of business that are not exclusively dedicated to the sale of sex (Choudhury et al., 2015). However, entertainment estab lishment workers such as singers, dancers, waitresses, masseuses, and bar workers have been found to engage in selling sex to men patronizing such places in order to supplement the womens income (World Health Organization, Regional Office for the Western Pacific, 2001). In the context of Nepal, Frederick et al.s (2010) noted that entertainment establishments such as cabin restaurants, 1 massage parlors, dance bars [not exotic dancing], and doohari [type of traditional singing style] restaurants often operate as a front for sex work. This article aims to explore the ways in which gender and power inequalities enable control over women work ing in entertainment establishments, and in turn the impact on their health. In Nepal, Risal and Hacker (2018) found that establishment entertainment workers are pri marily young women who have migrated to the capital and cite economic necessity for entering this work. Thus, the article is based on 35 in-depth interviews with young women who migrated to Kathmandu and currently work in various dance bars and restaurants centered on enter tainment. Gender and power inequalities are built from social repro ductive distinctions between human bodies (Connell, 2012). Thus, social institutions such as entertainment establishments are not gender neutral, and gender and power inequalities are constantly reproduced (Majic, 2014). In fact, gender and power inequalities are main tained through mechanisms such as unequal pay, discrimi natory practices, and an imbalance of control in the workplace that ensures women remain disempowered (Wingood & DiClemente, 2000). For instance, the boss/ employer has reward power, that is, paying a salary on time, offering a promotion, and controlling the pay scale as well as coercive power, that is, punishment such as fir ing and withholding of salary (see, for instance, five types of social power discussed by French & Raven, 1968). In addition, the women may also feel the imbalance of coer cive power between the client/customer and themselves where women may fear retaliation, abuse, and harassment (see, for instance, Choi & Holroyd, 2007). Data Collection

Thirty-five semistructured in-depth interviews were con ducted working with a local nongovernmental agency (NGO), Kumudini, in Kathmandu. Kumudini works to rescue women and children who have been exploited, abused, and trafficked. Academics often have to work with existing systems such as NGOs to locate and work with hard-to-reach or hidden populations (Benoit et al., 2005). The interview guide was created in collaboration with Kumudini, and the initial interview guide was pilot tested with women working in entertainment establish ments as well as with caseworkers from partner NGOs who provide services such as counseling, rehabilitation homes, and skills training to women working in the enter tainment establishments. The interview questions were finalized and grouped into the following subsections: background, working conditions, discussion about the profession, and health. The last category, health, was fur ther subdivided into sexual health, substance abuse, and mental health.

Due to the sensitive nature of this clandestine indus try, as well as to avoid being seen talking with outsiders by the people in the industry, three caseworkers who already had a working relationship with the women conducted the interviews. The caseworker approached potential participants, and if they agreed to be inter viewed, a time and a place convenient to the partici pants was set up. This maintained the privacy and safety of the participants and also enabled them to share their stories freely. Interviews lasted between one-and-a-half to two hours. The interviews were conducted and tran scribed verbatim in Nepali before being translated to English. All of this occurred in close collaboration with Kumudini.

Ethical Consideration

The study received ethical clearance from the Nepal Health Research Council, which is a government body in Nepal, as well as from a universitys institutional review board (IRB). The purpose of the research, the interview process, and the participants right to withdraw at any time were explained to the participants during their recruitment. Each participant was given a token of appre ciation of 150 rupees2 (~ US$2). At the start of the inter view, each participant was reminded that she could withdraw at any time if she did not wish to continue and she would still receive the token of appreciation. All par ticipants completed the interviews.

Participants

Thirty-five women were interviewed. At this point, it was determined that saturation had been met, because contin uous data analysis did not reveal new categories or dimensions, or subcategories of the categories that had already been developed from the data (Choudhury et al., 2015). The women ranged in age from 18 to 34 (M = 24.77, SD = 4.36). Out of the 75 districts in Nepal, the participants came from 22 different districts representing all the different ethnicities. The education level ranged from none to 10th grade (about one-third were unedu cated, about one-third had fifth-grade education level, and about one-third had studied up to 10th grade). The monthly income ranged from 3,000 rupees (~US$30) without tips to anywhere from 10,000 rupees (~US$97) to 25,000 rupees (~US$240) with tips.

Data Analysis

Thematic analysis guided the process of identifying, scrutinizing, and reporting patterns without trying to fit the data into a preexisting coding frame (Braun & Clarke, 2006). First, during open coding, transcripts were reviewed line by line to break down the data into smaller parts for comparison and further analysis (Corbin & Strauss, 2015). This resulted in 100 pages of phrases, words, and sentences. Next, axial coding was done by looking at the list of initial codes generated through open coding to identify patterns and relation ships between them and group them into categories (Corbin & Strauss, 2015). This grouping of the open codes resulted in five broad categories. Finally, selec tive coding occurred to further refine the categories and to better understand phenomena. During this stage, two things occurred: (a) refined categories and their accom panying quotes were read to finalize the theme (Corbin & Strauss, 2015), and (b) discussion between the local NGO partner and the researcher occurred to ensure accurate reflection of the data. Even though no formal respondent validation (Anderson, 2010) was done in terms of letting participants read through the data and provide feedback during the data analysis, constant clar ification was sought during the interviews to ensure that participants opinions and experiences were accurately interpreted as well as to ensure that the research ques tions were answered. Results

Sexual Harassment

The lived experiences of the participants revealed that sexual harassment by the male customers is a daily encounter in their workplace. Furthermore, revealed that men feel entitled to objectify womens bodies by demand ing sexual acts and expecting women to fulfill these demands. Exposure to daily sexual harassment affects the participants negatively and intensely and they feel sad, bad, and hurt. The participants come from low-income families, lack access to education, and, in turn, found themselves with limited options for employ ment. All participants noted supporting a family, whether child and/or parent and finding such work in these estab lishments out of economic necessity. However, this comes at the price of stigmatized labels such as whore and prostitute. For instance, a participant (18 years, working 10 months, ninth grade) explained,

When people hear that someone works in this [entertainment] sector they think people go there to do bad things at night. Outsiders think negatively about us. Even walking on the road, some call out whore or prostitute because they see us coming in and out of these places. Customers come to us at night but during the day, when they see us, they act as if we are not good because we work in these places.

Although the women were working as waitresses, danc ers [not exotic dancing], or singers, the stigmatized image of this industry impacted the ways in which they were viewed. In addition, this stigmatized image carries an expectation that the women are willing to engage in vari ous sexual activities. For example, a participant (18 years, working 9 months, sixth grade) explains: the guests3 tell us to sit with them. If we do not drink with them or do not do as they say [allowing touching] they bad mouth us with foul language. Using labels and verbal abuse are strategies that sustain gender and power inequalities in these establishments. For instance, another participant (27 years, working 4 years, high school) said,

Some guests come and directly ask you what your rate is and say, let us go out to a hotel today. They ask how much and sometimes they just pull your hands. I feel very sad when that happens. Some of them use inappropriate words and tell us that the intention of being a singer and a dancer is like that [i.e., sex worker] and they [women] intend to sell their prestige for their living.

Regardless of whether the women wishes to engage in sex work or not, they are caught between finding work as bargirls, waitresses, singers, and dancers and constantly fighting off sexual demands. As another participant (25 years, working 6 years, seventh grade) explained, Sometimes as soon as they arrive, they start touching you and ask straight away to spend the whole night and tell you how much they will pay. The sexual harassment is perpetuated through the notion of being impure and immoral women connected to the tarnished image that entertainment establishments carry in society. Another participant (19 years, working 2 years, ninth grade) high lighted the precarious positions of the women:

We have to serve [food] to the customer whether we know or do not know them; even when we do not want to talk them we have to serve them, smile at them, talk nicely to them. Some of them directly ask us to go out with them for the night by saying that they will give us this much or that much money. Some of them use vulgar words. All this we have to tolerate as an obligation of working there.

Despite encounters with daily sexual harassments through unwanted sexual advances and requests for unwanted sex ual acts, the participants must tolerate these instances to continue working. Touching/grabbing, demanding, and swearing reinforces the gender and power inequalities, where men feel entitled to demand sexual acts and expect women to fulfill their demands just because of where they work. This denies the existence of both the economic necessity to work there and limited options women have for other employment opportunities. Similarly, a partici pant (24 years, working 7 years, no education) said,

Some guests invite us to eat and drink with them, others ask you to go to with them to spend the night. Some try to touch your sensitive parts and pinch you too. If you try to stop them, some will say yeti pani chunuhunna talai [I cannot even touch you this much]. Touching, grabbing, pinching, asking to go with them to spend the night really hurts me, as I do not like such behavior.

Gender and power inequalities are maintained through unwanted demands such as whats wrong with you, why cant I even touch you this much. In fact, a participant (27 years, working 4 years, high school) said, My mind becomes tense, I feel very bad. I think how awful it is to be born a girl child in this world, when she encounters and has to tolerate sexual harassment. These instances are reminders of the gender and power inequalities in these establishments, which in turn makes the study partici pants feel helpless. Another participant (19 years, work ing 2 years, ninth grade) similarly said, I tolerate some of these situations, but I feel so bad and uneasy every time. I regret working in such a place and wish I could leave this place and work elsewhere. The need to work, combined with having limited options, exposes the women to sexual harassment by being part of an industry that carries a tarnished image in society. By default, the women are then expected to accept unwanted advances, demands, and remarks. Yet another participant (29 years, working 12 years, no education) further echoes this:

If you refuse to go with them or drink with them, they say you are working here but trying to act normal. They tell you, when you want, you will go with whomever you like but right now you are acting normal, whore, you are a whore.

The participants refusal being seen as a personal rejec tion further highlights the exercise of power by claiming the womans body as an object. As the experiences of the participants highlight, their desires are secondary to what the customers want and expect of the women. Objectification, power, and control of womens bodies is inherent to their ability to openly sexually harass without fear of consequences.

Professional Exploitation

Employers/owners of such establishments also exploit the precarious position of the women to further their own goals of driving profits. For instance, a participant (28 years, working 5 years, no education) said, our boss says that this work can be done only while you are young. If you get old, then he would fire us from the work. This is also evident in the fact that the average age of the partici pants in the study was 24. Furthermore, the women are not protected at work from the sexual harassment they encounter daily. A participant (22 years, working 2 months, fifth grade) explained,

One time someone tried to grab me, and I told him off nicely. Then he went and complained to the owner. He [the employer] did not do anything because we need to obey the rules, because if they [customers] call us to sit close to them, touch us, we cannot be angry. We are seen to be involved in that kind of work [sex work].

The tarnished image of the industry is exacerbated through the business owners who use coercive and reward power to exploit the women for their profit. For example, a participant (30 years, working 6 years, no education) said, the owners force us to go out with customers; oth erwise, they threaten to throw us out of work. It is diffi cult in this sector. I wish this would not happen. The customers also exploit the fact that women are unpro tected from sexual advances and harassments in these establishments. Similarly, another participant (34 years, working: 14 years, 10th grade) said,

We need to obey the rules because if they [customers] call us, touch us, we cant be angry. If we became angry whenever they [customer] touch us, then we can be fired from work.

Then, the customers dont come there and if the customers dont come, it is difficult to receive a salary. That is the rule of that place, we cannot be angry when the customers touch us and then we need to obey what they say, that is the rule.

Need to obey highlights the gender and power inequali ties inherent in these establishments that forces women to tolerate the harassment and exploitation in fear of losing their much-needed job. These experiences of professional exploitation reveals gendered social relations that enables the exercising of power and control over the womens bodies as pleasure objects for the sake of profit. To earn money is to tolerate in order to look after our family as one participant noted. Another participant (25 years, working 1 year, 10th grade) said,

The owner forces us to stay [in close proximity] with the customer. He insists that we make them drunk and talk mischievously about sex [engage in sexual innuendos]. If we do not agree then he will ask us not to come to work anymore.

Forces us is also a reference to the lack of choice of the women and control over their bodies as a commodity. The pressure to tolerate sexual harassment is directly related to driving profit for the business. In addition, there is also pressure to consume alcohol to further drive profit. One participant (24 years, working 7 years, no education) internalizes and excuses this control and exploitation of her employer by saying, I think it may be my shyness about talking with guests and my talking style with them. Now I improved my skills to talk with guests. However, another participant (29 years, working 10 years, no edu cation) explained,

Managers treat [them] differently and give more priority to those who sell more wine, alcohol and drinks, and they treat us like we are not important to them. They ask us to increase the sales by joining the guests and drinking with them, sitting with them [sexual talks]. When I said that I dont drink, then they used to reply that you are working here but acting like you dont drink and smoke.

Employers exercise coercive power by withholding sala ries and threatening to fire the women to ensure that their business thrives. The health of the women is inconse quential to the employers aim to increase profit. Playing on the fears of the women as part of exploiting them high lights the gender and power inequalities inherent in these establishments. The women are expected by both the cus tomers and employers to engage in sexual innuendos, to invite and to tolerate sexual advances, and to partake in daily alcohol consumption as part of their work. A par ticipant (22 years, working 1 year, fifth grade) further explained that drinking is incentivized as a form of reward. She said,

They [boss] insists that we drink as we work there, and we may have to drink even if we do not want to. It is necessary to drink daily in our work. If I drink one glass of wine, then when the customer pays their bill of 78 hundred rupees [~67 USD], we get the commission of that glass of wine, about one hundred fifty rupees [~1.5 USD].

Womens bodies are used both as an object of pleasure and a commodity, and as such, this perception creates room for the customers to become entitled to make sexual demands and advances. Furthermore, a participant (18 years, working 10 months, ninth grade) explained, Guests tell me that everyone drinks, so why dont you drink, big person? You dont want to be friends with us; you think you are superior to us. However, refusing to entertain sexual advances, not tolerating touching/grab bing, not engaging in sexual innuendos, and refusing to consume alcohol to get customers to spend more can result in the women losing their jobs, evidenced by thrown out. A participant (34 years, working 14 years, 10th grade) explains:

Even if we do not want to, the owner tells us to get off stage and go with the guests, and they are usually drunk and so behave and touch inappropriately. That makes me feel bad. If we tell them to stop, we will be thrown out of work. We will have nowhere to go tomorrow, so we cannot say anything.

These various strategies serve to disempower women which, in turn, serves to maintain the gender and power inequalities in the entertainment establishments. Thus, there is a tacit expectation that working in this environ ment means the women should be willing to be objecti fied as well as be a commodity. The manifestation of gender and power inequalities is underscored by the premise that women must be immoral to want to work in such an environment in the first place. A participant (18 years, working 10 months, ninth grade) said,

I think the customers should stop telling us that we were there for their use. I think that our salary should be given on time, and if we are sick, that days salary is deducted. It is too dangerous to walk in the street at night going back home, sometimes the police arrest us and sometimes people harass us. They [work] should provide transport. Most of all, I wish we would get some respect.

As illustrated by the participant, they are in a double bind: If they do not work, they do not have money and if they work, they have to give in to expectations of the custom ers and the employers harassment and exploitation. Although some employers may directly threaten the women about losing their jobs, others push their ulterior motive for more profit by incentivizing engagement in sexual innuendoes to sell more alcohol. Ultimately, the image of immorality of the entertainment establishment is used to exploit the women and sustain an environment of sexual harassment. These experiences have great implications for the womens physical, emotional, and psychological health and well-being.

Health Repercussions

Health and related health decisions made by the women is a reflection of the context that centers on maintaining gender and power inequalities. Exposure to increased physical health threats as well as to psychological and emotional strains have resulted, as the participants shared, in depression, insomnia, loneliness, anxiety, helplessness, and alcohol addiction, among other health concerns. For example, a participant (22 years, working 9 months, ninth grade) shared,

Before smoking, I did not have chest pains and I did not get cold and cough so often. When I started smoking, the first year I did not have any complaints. Nowadays, I smoke a lot and if I do not smoke at night [referring to work] I feel less pain.

As highlighted by the participant, the work conditions puts them at physical and mental health risk. Furthermore, another participant (27 years, working 4 years, 12th grade) highlights that the work environment creates pres sure on the women to give in to risky behavior despite knowing the health risks. She said, People who smoke and drink constantly surround us. Those who can control will control but those who cannot will pick up the habit. Given the professional exploitation that is geared around forcing risky health behaviors, control over not pick ing up smoking and drinking habits is difficult for the participants. This is further explained by a participant (34 years, working 14 years, 10th grade):

I never used to drink but I have to drink because of my work. Drink a little, they [the employer] urged, and I started to drink. They [the employer] says if you do not drink then there is no business and how can we pay you? I slowly started with one or two glasses but gradually it became a habit to drink.

Sitting with the client and drinking also implies that the women are expected to not only engage in this behaviors but also use their bodies to increase sales by tolerating and engaging in what the customer wants as illustrated in the earlier themes. Despite this, earlier themes also implied that the fear of losing their jobs or not receiving their salary prevents them from speaking up or walking away. However, this has repercussions not on only for the womens health but for their families as well. A partici pant (22 years, working 3 months, sixth grade) further explains:

By chance, if anyone gets ill because of compromising [referring to condom] and they do not have savings. It scares me to think. Most of us live far away from family. Living here means all the things have to be done by ourselves. If I get ill, who will look after me, anything may happen to me, then who will look after my child? I get stressed because of that, I am already getting stressed right now.

The participant implies that exposure to various health risks not only leaves her vulnerable but also increases the psychological stress of not knowing how her child will be cared for without her. The women face a lack of social support as well as lack of sufficient funds for treatment. As one participant explained, sometimes I have money and other times I dont. This then adds to the psychological burden as another participant (22 years, working 3 months, sixth grade) noted, If I go for treatment and medicine, with that money, I feel that I could have done something for my child instead. Furthermore, another participant (24 years, working 7 years, no education) said,

In this sector [entertainment establishments], alcohol and smoking are usual things. In addition to this, yes, some do have love affairs [euphemism for sex work] day by day, and such common things happen every day. I have seen these things develop into bad habits and friends who have fallen under the addiction to tobacco and alcohol.

As the participants highlighted, there is both an increased risk of adverse physical health outcomes as well as increased mental health struggles. Further illustrated is the fact that these women must constantly navigate high levels of health risks, often without help and support. A participant (25 years, working 3 years, no education) said, till now no one has [helped]. I console myself no matter how stressed I am. Similarly, another participant (25 years, working 6 years, seventh grade) elaborated,

I do not know what to do and where to turn for help. I often feel lonely and at that moment, I feel that it would be better to die than to live. But, I also thought to myself that dying doesnt make any good sense. If something happens to me, they would think that I did something wrong and my family would also be miserable. I also remind myself that there are so many other people who are suffering more than I am. This is how I comfort myself.

A negative mental state coupled with the lack of ability to afford health care and access to help increases loneliness, mental anguish, and anxiety, in addition to increasing the risk of adverse physical health outcomes. Although some are able to receive regular counseling through the local NGO, the stories revealed the increased level of risk to the physical, psychological, and mental health of women working in the nightlife industry. Similarly, a participant (23 years, working two and half years, eighth grade) noted a personal struggle that sums up the tension:

I wonder what kind of profession I have entered into. I feel like leaving the profession. Then I have to come back to work the next day. I think girls who work in this sector are treated as objects, who are open for use. They [employers] treat us like servants. They [customers] call us names and degrade us.

The exploitative work environment creates an environ ment for various health risks and this takes a toll on the women. This exploitative work environment also exac erbates gender and power inequalities which are exer cised openly by customers and employers, often disempowering women and such leaving her feeling lonely and helpless. In other words, power within the entertainment establishments contributes to structuring gender inequalities in the everyday interactions of the women in their workplace. A participant (27 years, work ing 4 years, high school) sums up the experience of working in this industry:

I have problems sleeping. I have various thoughts. I shared this problem with my sisters [others working girls] and they told me not to bring such bad thoughts to my mind as it may result in depression. These thoughts never happened to me in my life before working in this sector and I feel like something bad might happen. Some are also obliged to enter this sector. I wish people did not take it as negative profession.

Gender and power inequalities sustained in the entertain ment establishment has an adverse impact on the health of the women working there. The women occupy a lower status than men, that is, both customers and employers. This socially constructed gendered relationship is sus tained through power and dominance that objectifies and commodifies womens bodies. Sexual harassment and exploitation are strategies which then sustain these inequalities.

Discussion

Entertainment establishments present an elevated risk of violence and sexual exploitation, and thus, members of this group experience discrimination and stigma (Kelvin et al., 2013). This was illustrated in the finding that the dirty work, dirty women discourse found in sex work (Mavin & Grandy, 2013) is also experienced by women working as waitresses, singers, and dancers [not exotic dancing] in entertainment establishments. Exploring health risks in relation to gender and power inequalities allows us to raise broader questions with regard to wom ens health, and creates new options for prevention (Wingood & DiClemente, 2000). For instance, the findings of this study draws our attention to exploitation, harassment, verbal abuse, touching/grabbing, expecta tion of compliance, objectification, commodification, and labeling that presents various elevated risks to the women. Furthermore, these experiences exemplified unequal gender relations, mechanisms of power inequal ities, and negative health outcomes. Unlike other forms of sexual labor related to the sale of a service to satisfy a sexual fantasy, produce sexual excitement or arousal, and/or provide sexual satisfaction to the customer (Maticka-Tyndale et al., 2000), entertainment establish ment are primarily places of business that are not exclu sively dedicated to the sale of sex (Choudhury et al., 2015). Unlike other studies on entertainment establish ments (see, for instance, Choudhury et al., 2015; Kelvin et al., 2013; Risal & Hacker, 2018; Yang et al., 2010; Yi et al., 2015), this study did not specifically address the direct sale of sex but rather focused on exploring the existence of gender and power inequalities and its link to health. The findings suggest that experiences of sexual harassment and professional exploitation adversely affects the health of the women at the physical, emo tional, and psychological levels. Much of their health risks, conditions, and outcomes are then a product of their work environment.

The gendered exercise of power as the focal point of discussion about the health of the entertainment estab lishment workers enables us to recognize the conditions that create and sustain inequalities in the womens lives and the adverse impact on their health (Basnyat, 2017a; Wingood & DiClemente, 2000). The findings illustrated that verbal abuse, shaming, name calling, bullying, harassment, and exploitation are ways in which gender and power inequalities are exercised over women work ing in the entertainment establishments. Thus, focusing only on the individual-level behavior of the women such as promoting condom use and counseling to reduce abuse of alcohol does not adequately address the health risks and the adverse health outcomes created by their social environment (Basnyat, 2017b). For example, in Cambodia, a comprehensive community-based approach coupled with integrated HIV and sexual and reproduc tive health (SRH) services was implemented among women entertainment workers which resulted in a reduction in the prevalence of STI and induced abortion rates and an increase in the likelihood of seeking care and treatment for STI as well as contraceptive use (Yi et al., 2015). Thus, intervention programs need to take a multilevel approach that, in addition to individual level informational education, promotes a supportive social, normative, and working environment (Yang et al., 2010). It is important to pay attention to the ways in which gender interacts with labor, power, and relation ships and, in turn, focuses health interventions across the three domains of public health, social/behavioral sciences, and medicine (Wingood & DiClemente, 2000). Health and, in turn, health interventions then must be located within the multiple intersections of the womens lives rather than a standalone discussion of individual ized health decisions such as STI/HIV, condom negotia tion, limited partners, refraining from smoking, and alcohol use, all of which simply places the onus on the women themselves rather than understanding the con text within which their decisions are made (Basnyat, 2017a, 2017b).

Although this article focused on the institutional level of entertainment establishment, the lived experiences also revealed that gender and power inequalities are embedded at the societal level (i.e., lack of education and access to other job opportunities for women). Moreover, this article argues that understanding womens health sit uated within a social structure such as the entertainment establishment draws our attention to the multiple and simultaneous forms of inequalities often fostered through those structures. For instance, the institutional mecha nism that enables exploiting women through the exercise of power is rooted in a social system that prioritizes cer tain gendered norms and roles such as patriarchy and oppression (Basnyat, 2017b). These create adverse health risks, as the women shared, in terms of their physical, emotional, and psychological health and well-being. Furthermore, the findings reveal that gender should be understood in relation to power relations that sustain and perpetuate the inferior identity of entertainment estab lishment women workers. The findings also highlight that discussion of the interlinkages of gender, power, and health should be situated within the context that rein forces disempowering conditions for women.

Implications for Health Interventions

This article highlights the significance of the interlinkages between gender, power, and health for entertainment establishment workers, who are often relegated to work ing in an exploitative environment due to systematic inequalities such as patriarchy, lack of education, and access to jobs (Risal & Hacker, 2018). These findings highlight that social practices such as sexual harassment, incentivized exploitation, and invoking immorality are strategies through which gender and power inequalities are maintained within entertainment establishments. Furthermore, the findings illustrate that to address health risks within entertainment establishments, solutions must be located at the intersection of gender and power. The findings have the following implications for health inter ventions that are focused on changing individual health behaviors of the entertainment workers. First, the exis tence and interplay of gender and power inequalities raise the question of whether prevention advice focused at the individual level may be undermined in this context, thus limiting the womens ability to follow behavior-focused health promotion advice. Second, health advice solely focused on individual behavior risks fails to acknowledge the presence of health risks at the physical, emotional, and psychological levels, primarily stemming from the work environment within which the women are located. Third, health interventions that ignore the context of health risks privileges individual health risks centered on biological differences rather than the socially constructed gendered and power inequalities that dictate health behaviors. Fourth, the health of marginalized women cannot be addressed without also addressing the gendered inequali ties, labor division, power relations, and sexual norms that disadvantage women. Therefore, health interventions tar geting vulnerable, excluded, and marginalized women should begin with locating the discussion of health at these intersections rather than approaching health risks as a standalone individual-driven phenomenon.

Limitations and Conclusion

Exploring gender and power inequalities within enter tainment establishments enable us to recognize the soci etal conditions and patterns that disadvantages women and creates negative impact on health. Ultimately, the findings urge us to locate the discussion of the health of marginalized women within the socially constructed con text, while paying attention to the gendered inequalities, labor divisions, power relations, and proscribed sexual norms that impact health and health decision making.

The article has four major limitations. First, the participa tion was limited to entertainment workers who were acces- Notes sible through the collaborating NGOs. Although the lived

experiences may be representative of the entertainment establishment, the stories presented in the article only reflect those who were accessible through the NGO. Second, caseworkers prior relationship with the partici pants may have influenced the ways in which stories and experiences were shared during the interview. Future stud ies should consider the implications on disclosure of the participants, depending on their relationship with the inter viewer. Third, the article was limited to women working in the entertainment establishments and did not explore other subgroups such as call girls and escorts as well as various gender identities such as transgender who may also work at the establishments and/or use the establishments to solicit clients. Third, the article included women who have worked in this environment for an extensive time. It would be pertinent to also understand the experiences of those who are seeking to enter this work and/or have left this work and are reintegrating into society. These contexts can provide further nuanced understanding of gender, power, and health at the broader societal level.

In conclusion, the findings resonate with the shared experiences of violence, abuse, exploitation, and increased health risks in Western as well as Eastern enter tainment establishments (see, for instance, Choudhury et al., 2015; Maticka-Tyndale et al., 2000; Nemoto et al., 2008; Seib et al., 2009; Wong et al., 2012; Yi et al., 2015). The findings of this article extend the existing studies and highlight the significance of gender and power on health.
